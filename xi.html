Da Vinci Xi

Enhanced point of interaction for robotic surgery

Da Vinci Xi is a surgical robot developed by Intuitive Surgical, a global pioneer in robotic platforms for minimally invasive surgery. It enables surgeons to perform complex procedures by utilizing robotic instruments as natural extensions of their hands and eyes.

During the summer of 2016, I was an interaction design intern at the UX team of Intuitive Surgical. Through observations and interviews with surgical teams and clinical engineers, I identified challenge of effective interaction with Xi robot in the OR (operating room), especially for clinical staffs other than the surgeon, e.g. circulating nurses, scrub technicians and anesthetists. My design focused on those patient side assistants and how to promote seamless teamwork experience in the OR. After a few rounds of prototyping interactions and usability tests, I delivered a solution of enhanced interfaces for Xi robot and a tiered structure of support for OR users. The design was then translated into a storytellling demo featuring future user experience with surgical robots, which was widely received across design, engineering, production and marketing divisions.

Problem Statement

Effective communication has always been a challenge among different staffs in the OR; it becomes even more complicated when we bring robots into the surgical operation. While Intuitive Surgical has developed an amazingly sophisticated console for surgeons, other clinical staffs in the OR, for example, scrub tech or circulating nurses, have to rely on a screen that is far away from them to assist in the surgery. In addition, because the screen is original designed for surgeons, it's hard for assistants to associate information on the screen to the actual robot, which causes potential mismanipulationo of robotical arms or surgical instruments.

Observation and Interview

I identified 5 usability issues after spending two weeks observing and interviewing clinical engineers and surgical teams.

Unclear setup guidance
Current system provides a guided setup as a preparation step. Some users will skip it, and it is common for them to set up the surgical platform 180 degrees backward. Even they do follow the instruction, they may still select the wrong target anatomy.

Unnecessary struggle with laser pattern
The laser was designed for guiding the robot towards endoscope port. But the crosshair shape implies much more accuracy than necessary, and also implies some kind of orientation. Some users will struggle with the imperfection and end up spending a long time to make it perfect.

Incorrect arm association
50% arm-related troubleshooting processes involve manipulation on the external robot arm. But when the assistant try to resolve it, they will have to rely on a display that is far away from them. There is not always a one-to-one mapping from the screen to the 'real world', so they sometimes get confused and take incorrect actions.

Unclear troubleshooting guidance
With the only screen being far away, it is hard to put detailed and illustrated toubleshooting guidance there. Even it's provided, chances are that assistants don't immediately refer to it.

Interfaces unaccessible for non-sterile staffs
There is a clear divide between sterile and non-sterile fields in the operating room and staffs operate within certain area. Teamwork is challenging because staffs have limited access to information as well as limited interaction with the system.

Usability Test

I invited 18 participants with varying levels of experience with the robot as well as varying levels of clinical experience for a usability test. The new design eliminates arm misassociation problem and the average troubleshooting time is decreased by half. It also smooths the setup process with much more available and clearer instruction.
